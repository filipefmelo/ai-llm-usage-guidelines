Use the models. Own the output.

A shared, tool-agnostic guide for everyone working with large language models here. The principles hold whichever assistant you use. The responsibility stays with you.

01

Six principles that don't change

Whatever the tool, whatever the task. If you remember nothing else, remember these.

01

You own the output

The model assists. It does not take accountability. Your name is on the work, so the judgement has to be yours.

02

Verify before you trust

Treat every answer as a confident draft, not a fact. Check the numbers, the code, the citation. Models are fluent in being wrong.

03

Classify before you paste

Know what kind of data you are about to hand over. When in doubt, it is red. See the traffic light below.

04

Use sanctioned tools only

Work data goes into approved channels, never a personal account. Convenience is not a justification for shadow AI.

05

Keep a human in the loop

Scale oversight to risk. The bigger the blast radius, the closer the supervision. See the autonomy ladder.

06

Be transparent and explainable

Disclose AI assistance where it affects others, and be able to explain how a result was reached. If you cannot explain it, you cannot defend it.

02

Can I paste this?

The one question that matters most in a bank. Match what you are about to enter to the strictest category it touches.

Green

Free to use
  • Public information and documentation
  • Generic, boilerplate code with no secrets
  • Fully anonymised or synthetic data
  • General how-to and conceptual questions

Amber

Approved channel only
  • Internal, non-sensitive documents
  • Internal code without credentials or keys
  • Draft internal communications
  • Anonymised analyses and aggregates

Red

Never
  • Client data and any personal data (PII)
  • Account, payment or transaction detail
  • Credentials, keys, secrets, tokens
  • Anything under banking secrecy or legal privilege

Placeholder for governance. The exact boundary between amber and red, the list of approved channels, and the binding wording on banking secrecy (Art. 47 BankG) and FADP/revDSG are set by Legal, Compliance and the AI Circle. This card is the plain-language version, not the policy of record.

03

How much should the model do alone?

A shared vocabulary for delegation. The right level depends on what breaks if it goes wrong.

L1Assist

Suggest, you decide

Brainstorming, drafting, explaining. The model proposes; you write and own every word.

OversightYou check everything. Default for most everyday work.
L2Co-pilot

Produce, you review

The model generates code or content; nothing is used until you have read and understood it.

OversightReview every output before it ships. Review diffs, not vibes.
L3Supervised agent

Execute with checkpoints

Multi-step tasks run, but stop for your approval at defined gates and before any consequential action.

OversightApprove at checkpoints. Only for reversible, well-scoped tasks.
L4Autonomous

Runs unattended

Operates inside hard guardrails without step-by-step approval.

OversightOnly for low-risk, reversible, monitored, logged work. Requires sign-off.

The rule: permitted autonomy scales inversely with blast radius, data sensitivity and irreversibility.

04

How this goes wrong

The failure modes we have all seen. Naming them is half the defence.

✕
Shadow AI

Pasting work data into a personal account because it was faster. Speed is not a policy.

✕
Prompt and pray

Accepting output with no review step. The review step is the work.

✕
Shipping what you can't explain

If you could not have written it without the model, you are not yet ready to ship it.

✕
Confidence as correctness

A fluent, certain answer is not a verified one. Hallucination sounds exactly like expertise.

✕
Over-automating the irreversible

Letting an agent take actions that cannot be undone without a human gate.

✕
Letting the model set the requirements

It is good at answers, not at deciding what the question should have been.

05

Getting better results

Practical habits. Pick your lane.

01

Give it a role and context

"You are a compliance reviewer. Here is the draft. Flag risks." Context beats cleverness.

02

Show one good example

One sample of the tone or format you want teaches more than three paragraphs of instruction.

03

Ask for the format you need

Table, bullet list, three options, 80 words. Say it up front and you save a round trip.

04

Iterate in small steps

Refine one thing at a time. Big rewrites hide the change you actually wanted.

05

Make it critique itself

"What is weak about this answer?" often surfaces the flaw you would have missed.

06

Never assume it knows our facts

It does not know our products, our clients or our numbers. If it states one, you provided it or it invented it.

01

Give it the real context

Relevant files, interfaces, constraints and the existing patterns. A model with no repo context guesses.

02

Ask for a plan before code

Have it outline the approach first. Cheaper to correct a plan than a diff.

03

Specify constraints and tests

State the contract: inputs, edge cases, the test that must pass. Better still, write the test first.

04

Review diffs, not output

Read what changed, line by line. git diff is your review surface, not the chat window.

05

Keep secrets out of the loop

No keys, tokens or prod data in prompts, configs or context files. Assume the transcript is logged.

06

Pin and log the model

Record which model and version produced what. Reproducibility and audit start here.
